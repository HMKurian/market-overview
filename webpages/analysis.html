Fundamental Analysis





Fundamental analysis calculates the original or natural value of a company share. Measuring the intrinsic value of a company is based on several factors. Such as the current financial situation of the company, the economic condition of the respective county, etc.



Sources





Fundamental analysis can be taken from publicly available data such as, from 8-K, 10-Q and 10-K. Analysts also use a company's revenue, earnings, future growth, return on equity, and profit. Investors always assume that the stock market will reflect its fundamental value. Sometimes, different analysts focus on different elements and the prices they predict will vary. Investors will try to buy stocks below the intrinsic values posted by the analysts.



Quantitative



information including numbers and formulas.

- Balance sheet = (assets = liabilities + shareholders' equity)

- Income statement

- Cash flows



Qualitative



Understanding value of a company using its quality and standard.

- Business model

- Competition

- Management

- Industry

Technical Analysis





Technical analysis is done through observing price movement and volume of a stock. Traders use technical analysis to evaluate an entry point and exit point. Technical analysis is often done through watching indicators. Mostly analysts looks at

- Price trends

- Chart patterns

- Volume and momentum indicators

- Moving averages

- Support and resistance levels

Technical analysts believe that history is bound to repeat itself. Stock prices fluctuate based on sentiment and chart patterns often reflect the emotions of traders.



The Principles



1) Identify the present range and predict what the next range will be

2) Plan early what price is suitable to enter. Looking for patterns and using the trend will help traders to maximize their profit.

3) “The trend is your friend…until it bends” - trend is likely to follow a pattern.

4) “Trend goes up and down - when stocks make a sharp move buyers experience FOMO (Fear of missing out) and try to get in by practically paying any price. However once the last buyers buys the stock, the market tends to experience a downwards momentum and sell orders increase.

5) The stock price is likely to follow the same direction

Relative Strength Index

The Relative Strength Index (RSI indicator) measures the strength and change of price movements. This indicator was developed by J. Welles Wilder. The RSI tells you whether it is a good time to enter into a stock at a given price. The RSI ranges between 0 and 100. If the RSI ranges above 70, it indicates that the stock is overbought. If it oscillates below 30, it indicates that it is oversold. If the stock is overbought, the price tends to go downwards. If the stock is oversold, the price tends to move upwards.

The ranges vary between a bull and bear market. During bull markets, prices tend to be 40 to 90 (40; oversold and 90 overbought). During bear markets, prices tend to be in the 10 to 60 range(10 oversold and 60 overbought).





MACD

The MACD indicator is primarily used to trade trends. It is not usually used to identify over bought or oversold conditions. If the MACD crosses above the 0 line, the stock price is considered bullish. If it crosses below the zero line, it is considered bearish.



Accumulation/Distribution Line

This indicator was developed by Mark Chaikin who is also famous for creating the Chaikin Oscillator. It provides insight whether money is coming into or out of a security at a given time. If stock is being accumulated(bought) price will go higher. If it is distributed(sold) price will decrease.
